Yoasobi (夜遊び)


Yoasobi is a Japanese pop duo formed in 2019 by Sony Music Entertainment Japan. It composed of Vocaloid producer Ayase and singer-songwriter Lilas Ikuta. The duo's name Yoasobi originates from the Japanese word yoasobi (夜遊び), which means "nightlife".

Ayase, a singer-songwriter, Vocaloid producer, musician, arranger, and keyboardist and then-vocalist of rock band Davinci (2012–2020). He was born in Ube, Yamaguchi Prefecture, Japan in April 4, 1994. He began using Vocaloid software Hatsune Miku to produce music and uploaded his first song, "Sentensei Assault Girl".

Lilas Ikuta or Ikuta was born in Japan and moved to Chicago, Illinois, USA shortly after and lived there until the age of three. She is a singer-songwriter, a finalist in “SINGIN’JAPAN” sponsored by Sony Music and a member of the cover group Plusonica. She participated in the new artist training course, called the Lesson, which was sponsored by Sony Music Entertainment Japan, and released her demo CD 15 no Omoi.

During the COVID-19 pandemic in Japan, Yoasobi's debut single, "Yoru ni Kakeru", gave the duo the breakthrough by spending six non-consecutive weeks atop Billboard Japan Hot 100 and topping its 2020 year-end chart, as well as receiving the first ever diamond certification for streaming from the Recording Industry Association of Japan. It first took off on the short video platform TikTok and other social media, leading Spotify’s Viral Top 50 (Japan) ranking in January.

It was followed by several successful singles, including Blue Period-inspired "Gunjō", and anime series themes: "Kaibutsu" for Beastars (2021), "Shukufuku" for Mobile Suit Gundam: The Witch from Mercury (2022), "Idol" for Oshi no Ko (2023), and "Yūsha" for Frieren (2023).

Think of their debut single “Yoru ni Kakeru,” a song telling the tragic tale of a boy who unwillingly gave in to the temptation of Thanatos while pursuing the girl he once thought he had saved from death. Or their hit “Idol,”a song about the inner monologue of an idol navigating the multi-layered façades of Japan’s entertainment industry. Or even “Biri-Biri,” their latest release, based on the short novel After the Rain with You made to commemorate the first anniversary of Pokémon Scarlet and Violet.

YOASOBI manages to encapsulate the wide range of emotions inherent in such a vast catalog of stories like no other in the music industry. And the same goes with the different genres in which these tales materialize.
